My "Write Your New York" Essay on the Lower East Side

"False Truth and Change in New York City"

By Matthew B.

People who have not lived most of their lives in New York City have built an idea of spectacle and glamor around this city, they grab these ideas from the long list of media about this city in the forms of art and film. But in reality these ideas couldn’t be farther from the truth.

The media glamorizes New York City for its architectural feats, bustling nature, and unfriendly inhabitants, particularly in Manhattan, specifically in film. Constantly using Manhattan buildings as a set for scenes in films, justified since the borough contains several of the most famous landmarks and areas of the city. The incorporation of a famous landmark such as the Empire State Building or Times Square is nearly essential. These locations are captivating: the Empire State Building is widely recognized for its beautiful art deco architecture style, Times Square for its bustling and crowded environment. Locations like these appear frequently in movies and television.

However, rather than serving as the true small fragments of the city these many “spectacle” locations serve as the entire picture of the city in film which can be deceiving for viewers and steals the light from the real New York City. The reality of New York City is not found where flashing billboard advertisements for Coca Cola beaming at dark, but where the only source of illumination in the night is street lights and barber shop poles the smog of manhole covers in the night. The Lower East Side and Alphabet City are particular examples of the true New York City environment though lacking the glamorous qualities of what is seen in films or tourist sites. Both neighborhoods have no overpriced gift shops selling shirts and keychains, rather in the Lower East Side there are established shops and restaurants. Places with deep roots in the culture of the footsteps that stood there before. The Lower East Side and Alphabet City which might not be wealthy areas, but they are rich in culture. Despite lacking the appeal of tourists these neighborhoods can be equally, if not more, captivating. The drawing scent of the many delicious small local restaurants can be smelled from down the entire block. establishments that have filled bellies and whose canopies have shaded the dry concrete paths, owned and passed down by families, found in abundance. There are several parks scattered throughout, bringing joy and fulfillment revisited periodically by anyone nearby old or young. Staple corner shops and delis on every turn who trust that you will pay them when you are short on cash. Barber shops where you heard people have made appointments despite the long trip from Staten Island, and you look at them crazy until you understand that there is a level of connectedness and familiarity here you won’t find anywhere else.

But no tourists or film makers want to go over to places like Raul’s Barber Shop to get a cut, or buy a slice from Ray’s Pizza, or eat from the Chinese Restaurant, or shop at the Essex Retail Market. They want to go to the high and mighty thick buildings that coat the skyline and fill them with awe. Because of that all these places are gone. Raul’s Barber Shop got shut down because rent got too high, same with Ray’s Pizza and the Chinese Restaurant. Now Ray’s Pizza and the Chinese Restaurant have been uprooted, reduced to graves now being built over, gone and replaced by scaffolding, and soon to become another solid color plain textured clone building in this neighborhood attempting to mimic the simplistic style that draws the appeal of tourists. Raul’s Barber Shop is hollowed out, stripped of its roots, and has been waiting to be rented out for 2 years. I can still remember going to many places that are no longer here. I remember going to the Essex Retail Market with my grandparents and getting hit with the potent stench of different meats. I remember the market shrinking in size with each visit, until I never went again. Now it’s relocated to a big disgusting plain cube plastered with QR codes and oversimplified logos. And like it other well cherished robust buildings are now depleted to hollow shells eventually rented out to serve the same purpose, but never delivering the same sensations to the community. Everytime I pass these “shells” I think about what could have been if people cared about them a little more.

But I suppose this drastic change is what New York City is. In this constantly evolving concrete jungle of a city the thin towering or stout buildings that inhabit these streets are built and enjoyed by many for long periods of time. People go to their favorite deli or corner store to grab a bite, places that have been there for as long as they can remember. They walk the same streets, pass the same towers and concrete walls, all on their way to school or work. They tread streets that have always been there, streets that they have always known. Yet buildings get torn down and new ones are built. The streets of life we walk are evolved by the removal of buildings that are no longer needed. Rather than get upset at what's gone, we should cherish what we have. We should stop by that shop once in a while and give them business so they can keep the lights on for more days, and suggest for other people to come to the places you love. Bring attention to the hidden truth inside of these buildings and streets, and steal the spotlight from the tallest buildings.
